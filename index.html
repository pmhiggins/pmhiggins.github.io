Pete Higgins

Astrobiology PhD student, University of Edinburgh, UK

I'm in the final year of my PhD in Astrobiology at the University of Edinburgh. My research is focussed on assessing planetary habitability using computational methods and spacecraft/laboratory data. With this, we can estimate whether any form of life could survive or even thrive based purely on local chemistry, geology, and physical conditions. I have led supervision of BSc / MSc student projects, and have some experience teaching and lecturing on undergraduate level courses.

I completed my MSci in Natural Sciences at Lancaster University in 2017. I focussed on physics and chemistry, with my masters thesis on neutral atom dynamics in Neptune's magnetosphere.

Projects

NutMEG

NutMEG [Nutrients, Maintenance, Energy and Growth] is a python module for predicting the growth behaviour of microbial organisms in astrobiology. It’s designed to estimate the energetic availability through local chemistry and the cost of defending against extreme or adverse conditions to work out whether an environment could be habitable and how biology could behave there. You can check out NutMEG's documentation [mirror], or go straight to the code on GitHub.

Surviving in Extremes

The more 'extreme' an environment is, the more difficult it is to survive in. For example, at high temperatures or adverse pH it becomes more difficult for life to maintain the structure of important biomolecules like proteins and DNA.

One method I employ to predict the energetic costs of survival is considering the potential adverse effects of the local environment. The cumulative energy costs of multiple extremes and poor microbial adaptation results in large deficits to overcome, as shown in the animation - just one combination of several potential adaptations NutMEG can simulate.

Enceladus

Enceladus has a large subsurface ocean of water, and the Cassini spacecraft’s observations point to the existence of CH4, CO2, and H2 - the ingredients of the methanogenesis metabolism - as well as various salts, silicates and alkanes. These make it a primary candidate in for extraterrestrial life in the solar system. However, other key parameters of the ocean which we need to know to determine its habitability are difficult to calculate, such as the pH and composition. I have been using NutMEG to predict what possible combinations of Enceladus' parameter space could be habitable to life, as-we-know-it or otherwise.

The figure here shows an early estimation of the energy available via methanogenesis and the energy conservable by a typical methanogen in an Enceladean ocean at pH 8.6 which I have presented at numerous conferences. Our predictions have come a long way since this - on to biomass and biosignature estimates - so check back for updates!
