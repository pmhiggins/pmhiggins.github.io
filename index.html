Peter M. Higgins

Astrobiologist, University of Toronto, Canada

I'm a Postdoctoral Fellow in the Stable Isotope Lab at the University of Toronto. I examine how thermodynamic principles control whether or not life can survive, grow and/or evolve in various physico-chemical settings. To do so, I develop and employ computational models which: i) assess dynamic habitability, ii) constrain possible biomass levels, and iii) estimate biosignature production and detectability against an abiotic background. The universal nature of these laws allows us to examine environments throughout the cosmos, from deep earth through deep time, to the oceans of icy moons in the outer solar system, and even exoplanets around distant stars.

Projects

NutMEG

NutMEG [Nutrients, Maintenance, Energy and Growth] is a python module for predicting the growth behaviour of microbial organisms in astrobiology. It’s designed to estimate the energetic availability through local chemistry and the cost of defending against extreme or adverse conditions to work out whether an environment could be habitable and how biology could behave there. You can check out NutMEG's documentation [mirror], or go straight to the code on GitHub. At the moment there is a problem with the generation of NutMEG's API on readthedocs, you can access it here!

Surviving in Extremes

The more 'extreme' an environment is, the more difficult it is to survive in. For example, at high temperatures or adverse pH it becomes more difficult for life to maintain the structure of important biomolecules like proteins and DNA.

One method I employ to predict the energetic costs of survival is considering the potential adverse effects of the local environment. The cumulative energy costs of multiple extremes and poor microbial adaptation results in large deficits to overcome, as shown in the animation - just one combination of several potential adaptations NutMEG can simulate.

Icy moons

Enceladus has a large subsurface ocean of water, and the Cassini spacecraft’s observations point to the existence of CH4, CO2, and H2 - the ingredients of the methanogenesis metabolism - as well as various salts, silicates and alkanes. These make it a primary candidate in the search for extraterrestrial life in the solar system. However, other key parameters of the ocean which we need to know to determine its habitability are difficult to calculate, such as the pH and composition. I have been using NutMEG to predict what possible combinations of Enceladus' parameter space could be habitable to Earth-like life.

The figure here shows an early estimation of the energy available via methanogenesis in an Enceladean ocean when heated to different temperatures (Higgins et al., 2021). Our predictions have come a long way since this - on to biomass and biosignature estimates - so check back for updates!
